Internal Revenue Code Section 162 — Trade or Business Expenses

(a) In general. There shall be allowed as a deduction all the ordinary and necessary expenses
paid or incurred during the taxable year in carrying on any trade or business, including—
(1) a reasonable allowance for salaries or other compensation for personal services actually
rendered;
(2) traveling expenses (including amounts expended for meals and lodging other than amounts
which are lavish or extravagant under the circumstances) while away from home in the pursuit
of a trade or business; and
(3) rentals or other payments required to be made as a condition to the continued use or
possession, for purposes of the trade or business, of property to which the taxpayer has not
taken or is not taking title or in which he has no equity.

The Supreme Court has interpreted the "ordinary and necessary" standard on multiple occasions.
In Welch v. Helvering, 290 U.S. 111 (1933), the Court held that an expense is "necessary" if
appropriate and helpful for the development of the business, and "ordinary" if it is a common
and accepted practice in the taxpayer's line of business, even if it happens only once in the
taxpayer's own experience. Whether an expenditure is deductible under Section 162 or must
instead be capitalized turns on whether it produces a benefit that extends substantially beyond
the taxable year, as addressed in INDOPCO, Inc. v. Commissioner, 503 U.S. 79 (1992).

Illegal payments and expenses that frustrate a sharply defined public policy are not deductible
even if ordinary and necessary in the trade, consistent with the reasoning in Commissioner v.
Sullivan, 356 U.S. 27 (1958), which nonetheless permitted deduction of rent and wages paid in
connection with an illegal bookmaking operation because those particular expenses did not
themselves violate the law.
